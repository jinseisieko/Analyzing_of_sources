# Literature Review

## SBOM Tool Accuracy and Vulnerability Detection Performance
SBOM tool accuracy has emerged as a critical concern for software supply chain security. Multiple empirical studies have evaluated SBOM generators across different ecosystems, revealing significant variations in tool performance. Benedetti et al. [1] conducted a comprehensive comparison of five SBOM tools (cdxgen, GH-sbom, ORT, Syft, and Trivy) on 1,000 Python packages, finding that cdxgen achieved the best baseline performance with 49.77% Jaccard similarity, while their proposed PIP-sbom reached 78.39%. Similarly, Haigitler and Merli [3] evaluated SBOM tools across four programming languages (Python, C, Rust, TypeScript), concluding that cdxgen performed best for Python (7/12 criteria) but no single tool fulfilled all requirements across languages. Rabbi et al. [4] compared four SBOM tools (ORT, cnn, Syft, cdxgen) across 50 projects for JavaScript/npm ecosystems, finding that cnn achieved the highest accuracy (96.29%) while ORT exhibited the lowest performance (74.06% accuracy). Notably, they reported that ORT and Syft did not provide dependency identification capabilities, significantly limiting their utility for software supply chain analysis [4]. These findings align with Imtiaz et al. [2], who compared nine SCA tools on OpenMRS and found vulnerability counts varied widely (17–332 for Maven, 32–239 for npm), concluding that practitioners should not rely on any single tool.

## False Positives and False Negatives in SBOM Generation
The literature consistently identifies false positives and false negatives as major challenges in SBOM-based vulnerability assessment. Benedetti et al. [1] reported that baseline SBOM tools generated approximately ten times more false positives than their proposed solution (978 FP for cdxgen vs. 47 FP for PIP-sbom), with 75% of dependencies listed in SBOMs not actually installed during package installation. This overestimation creates an investigation burden that may discourage SBOM adoption [1]. Furthermore, Ponta et al. [11] and [13] demonstrated that metadata-based vulnerability detection tools produced substantial false positives because metadata often lacked completeness, consistency, or availability. Their code-centric approach using Eclipse Steady found that 88.8% of OWASP DC findings were false positives, while manual review confirmed 100% of Steady's code-based findings as true positives [13]. However, they acknowledged limitations: the code-centric approach could not detect vulnerabilities fixed by configuration changes rather than code modifications, and the authors implemented reachability analyses only for Java [13]. Pashchenko et al. [10] found that approximately 20% of dependencies with known vulnerabilities remained undeployed and therefore attackers could not exploit them, confirming that traditional approaches significantly over-inflate vulnerable dependency counts by failing to distinguish deployed from non-deployed dependencies. Consequently, this distinction is crucial for avoiding wasted effort on vulnerabilities that attackers could not exploit in practice [10].

## Tool Evolution and Version Tracking Gaps
A critical gap in the literature concerns the longitudinal assessment of SBOM tool performance across versions. Multiple studies compare tools at single points in time [1][2][3][4], yet none track how tool accuracy changes across major releases. Imtiaz et al. [2] explicitly noted that the researchers executed all tools during September 2020 to ensure fair comparison, acknowledging that vulnerability data updated over time but not examining how tool updates affect detection rates. Haigitler and Merli [3] used specific tool versions for reproducibility (Syft 0.90.0, Cdxgen 9.7.5) but did not assess whether newer versions improved accuracy. Rabbi et al. [4] assessed reproducibility by generating SBOMs twice for each project but excluded serialNumber and timestamp fields researchers expected to vary, without examining version-to-version performance changes. Moreover, the SBOM drift literature highlights why version tracking matters. Thirumalaisamy [8] argued that SBOM accuracy deteriorated rapidly in DevSecOps environments due to dynamic changes in dependencies and build processes, with automatic dependency resolution by package managers causing undocumented SBOM drift. However, this study remains conceptual and methodological without empirical quantitative results on tool version performance [8].

## Ecosystem-Specific Considerations
The literature reveals significant ecosystem-specific variations in SBOM tool performance. For Python, Benedetti et al. [1] identified two root causes for poor SBOM tool performance: inadequate package manager support and failure to model actual dependency collection behavior. They concluded that native integration of SBOM generation into package managers significantly improved vulnerability assessment accuracy [1]. For JavaScript/npm, Zimmermann et al. [12] analyzed 5.3 million package versions and found that installing an average npm package introduced implicit trust in approximately 79 third-party packages and 39 maintainers. They reported that up to 40% of all packages depended on code with at least one publicly known vulnerability, with approximately two-thirds of security advisories remaining unpatched [12]. Decan et al. [15] compared seven packaging ecosystems and found npm exhibited exponential growth in both packages and dependencies (R² ≥ 0.92), with median dependent packages having far more transitive dependencies than direct dependencies (21 vs. two for npm) [15].

## Stakeholder Perspectives on SBOM Quality
Stakeholder studies reveal widespread concerns about SBOM quality and tool sufficiency. Stalnaker et al. [5] surveyed 150 practitioners and found 33% of SBOM consumers mentioned poor quality SBOMs as a challenge, while 33% of critical OSS contributors mentioned SBOM verifiability as a major challenge. They identified 12 major challenges facing SBOM creation and use, including inaccurate and incomplete SBOMs [5]. Xia et al. [6] conducted interviews with 17 practitioners and surveys with 65 respondents, finding that practitioners generally neglected SBOM data validation and SBOM generation required further standardization. They concluded that SBOM adoption was still in early stages despite significant benefits for supply chain transparency [6].

## Critical Analysis and Research Positioning
The literature demonstrates strong agreement on several points: (1) SBOM tool accuracy varies significantly across tools and ecosystems [1][2][3][4], (2) false positives and negatives are pervasive problems [1][11][13], (3) no single tool fulfills all requirements [3][5], and (4) ecosystem-specific factors significantly influence tool performance [1][12][15]. However, notable disagreements and limitations exist. Rabbi et al. [4] found Syft lacked dependency identification capabilities for npm, while Benedetti et al. [1] included Syft in their Python evaluation without noting this limitation, suggesting ecosystem-specific functionality differences. Additionally, while Ponta et al. [11][13] advocated code-centric approaches as superior to metadata-based methods, limitations restricted their approach to Java and could not detect configuration-based vulnerabilities [13]. The most valid and reliable findings come from studies with large sample sizes and clear methodology: Benedetti et al. [1] (1,000 Python packages, 95% confidence level, 3.04% margin of error), Zimmermann et al. [12] (5.3 million package versions), and Decan et al. [15] (seven ecosystems, 830,000 packages). These studies provide statistically robust evidence of SBOM tool limitations.

## Contribution to Literature
This research addresses a critical gap identified across multiple sources: the absence of version-longitudinal measurement of SBOM tool accuracy. While Benedetti et al. [1], Rabbi et al. [4], and Haigitler and Merli [3] provide valuable cross-sectional comparisons, none examine how tool updates affect false negative rates over time. This is particularly important given Thirumalaisamy's [8] findings on SBOM drift and Xia et al.'s [6] observation that SBOM tooling requires improvement. This study will track Syft, Trivy, and cdxgen across four major release versions (2022–2025) for 100 Python and JavaScript projects to: (1) provide the first longitudinal assessment of whether tool evolution improves or degrades SBOM completeness, (2) identify whether regressions occur between versions (addressing Stalnaker et al.'s [5] concern about SBOM quality consistency), and (3) enable practitioners to make informed decisions about tool version selection (addressing Imtiaz et al.'s [2] recommendation against relying on single tools without version context). This research synthesizes the cross-sectional comparison methodology from Benedetti et al. [1] and Rabbi et al. [4] with longitudinal tracking, contributing new knowledge about SBOM tool evolution patterns that current literature cannot provide due to its static evaluation approach.
