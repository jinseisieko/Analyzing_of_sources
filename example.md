# Literature Review (Verified & Corrected)

## SBOM Adoption and Current Practice
The Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) has emerged as a critical component for software security and supply chain management, particularly following regulatory mandates such as the 2021 US Executive Order [7]. Despite increased awareness, with over 80% of surveyed organizations aware of the mandate, SBOMs remain an early technology not yet adequately adopted in practice [5], [6]. Stakeholders report significant challenges, including insufficient tooling and concerns regarding SBOM integrity and validation [6]. Surveys indicate that 33% of SBOM consumers face challenges with inaccurate and incomplete SBOMs, while 33% of critical OSS contributors cite verifiability as a major challenge [5]. Furthermore, while 90% of organizations have started or are planning their SBOM journey, the pressure to maintain SBOMs primarily targets industry at the end of the supply chain, leaving upstream projects with little incentive [5], [6]. This landscape establishes a pressing need for reliable automated generation tools, yet empirical evidence suggests current solutions are inconsistent.

## Variability in Tool Accuracy and Completeness
Empirical evaluations of SBOM and Software Composition Analysis (SCA) tools reveal significant variability in performance across ecosystems and toolsets. In the Python ecosystem, Benedetti et al. [1] found that state-of-the-art tools are often unsuitable for proper vulnerability assessment, with baseline tools listing dependencies that are not actually installed in approximately 75% of cases. Similarly, in the JavaScript npm ecosystem, Rabbi et al. [4] evaluated four SBOM generation tools (ORT, cnn, Syft, and cdxgen), finding that while cdxgen achieved high recall for component versions (92.57%), cnn demonstrated superior overall accuracy (96.29% for component names, 99.97% precision), and Syft and ORT lacked dependency identification features entirely [4]. Haigitler and Merli [3] corroborated these completeness issues across multiple languages, noting that tools like Syft and cdxgen failed completeness criteria for system-level languages (C), although performance varied by language [3].

This inconsistency extends beyond SBOM generators to broader SCA tools. Imtiaz et al. [2] compared 9 industry-leading SCA tools on OpenMRS, finding that the count of reported vulnerable dependencies varied widely from 17 to 332 for Maven projects across the studied tools. They concluded that practitioners should not rely on any single tool, as this can result in missing known vulnerabilities [2]. Collectively, these cross-sectional studies establish that while tools exist, their ability to generate complete and accurate SBOMs is inconsistent, ecosystem-dependent, and prone to significant divergence in vulnerability reporting [1], [2], [3], [4].

## Root Causes of Inaccuracy: Metadata, Dependencies, and Reachability
The literature identifies several underlying causes for the inaccuracies observed in SBOM generation and subsequent vulnerability scanning. A recurring theme is the reliance on metadata rather than code-centric analysis. Ponta et al. [11] argue that metadata-based vulnerability detection tools often produce false negatives or false positives because metadata are frequently incomplete or inconsistent. This is supported by findings from Eclipse Steady, where 88.8% of OWASP Dependency-Check findings were false positives due to imprecise library matching [13]. Conversely, code-centric approaches that determine reachability of vulnerable code through combined static and dynamic analysis are robust against these false positives [11], [13].

Beyond metadata limitations, the complex structure of dependency networks exacerbates accuracy issues. Zimmermann et al. [12] analyzed the npm ecosystem, finding that installing an average package introduces implicit trust in approximately 79 third-party packages, creating a large attack surface where up to 40% of all packages depend on code with at least one publicly known vulnerability [12]. Pashchenko et al. [10] highlight that traditional dependency analysis often over-inflates vulnerability counts by failing to distinguish between deployed and non-deployed dependencies, noting that approximately 20% of dependencies affected by known vulnerabilities are not deployed and therefore cannot be exploited [10]. These studies indicate that inaccuracies are not merely static errors but are compounded by transitive dependency complexity and the inability of tools to discern actual usage [10], [12].

## Temporal Dynamics: Drift and Ecosystem Evolution
While cross-sectional accuracy is well-documented, the temporal stability of SBOM tools and the ecosystems they scan remains less explored. Thirumalaisamy [8] conceptualizes "SBOM drift," where the actual artifact contents diverge from the declared SBOM over time due to automatic dependency updates and build environment variations, leading to rapid deterioration of SBOM accuracy in DevSecOps environments [8]. This drift is compounded by the evolution of the packaging ecosystems themselves. Decan et al. [15] analyzed dependency network evolution across seven packaging ecosystems, observing exponential growth in npm and CRAN, while Cargo and CPAN exhibited linear growth patterns (with R² ≥ 0.97 for linear fits) [15]. They noted that the ratio of transitive to direct dependencies increases over time in several ecosystems, and that a minority of packages accounts for most updates, creating "fragile" packages vulnerable to failure propagation [15]. While Thirumalaisamy [8] discusses accuracy deterioration conceptually, and Decan et al. [15] track ecosystem growth, neither provides empirical measurement of tool-specific False Negative Rates (FNR) across tool versions.

## Research Gap and Proposed Contribution
The absence of version-longitudinal measurement creates a critical knowledge gap. Practitioners cannot assess whether updating SBOM tools consistently reduces False Negative Rates or if regressions occur between major releases. Existing studies confirm that tools like Syft, Trivy, and cdxgen vary in accuracy [1], [4], and that SBOM completeness is essential for vulnerability mitigation [3]. However, no study has tracked the FNR of these specific tools across multiple major releases (e.g., 2022–2025) on a consistent set of projects. Most empirical evaluations are cross-sectional, capturing tool performance at a single point in time [1], [2], [3], [4], while longitudinal work focuses on ecosystem evolution rather than tool evolution [15]. This research proposal addresses this void by conducting a longitudinal analysis of FNR changes in Syft, Trivy, and cdxgen. By synthesizing the cross-sectional accuracy benchmarks established in prior work [1], [3], [4] with a temporal analysis of tool evolution, this study will determine whether tool development trajectories align with the industry need for improved SBOM reliability [5], [6].
